Product Taste

When AI can build anything, knowing what to build is the superpower.

Point of View

Strong opinion that differentiates. Nobody is neutral on the Cybertruck.

Craft

Obsessive care for detail. The opposite of AI slop.

Empathy

Solve the emotional need, not just the functional one.

Delight

Belief #1
"Front-load all research before building"

Building was expensive. One wrong bet could waste a quarter. So we front-loaded certainty: weeks of research before greenlighting anything.

When a prototype takes hours, not months, you build to learn, not learn to build.

🎬 DEMO 2: Iterative Prototyping (~5 min)

Show 4 prompt iterations building on each other - a PM shipping real UI without writing code.

  2. Prompt 2: "Add qualifier-based search - author:name, engine:copilot, has:skills, keyword:tag, category:name. Support negation with - prefix. Allow optional spaces after the colon."
  3. Prompt 3: "When search is focused and empty, show a help panel listing available qualifiers. As user types, switch to autocomplete. Hide help once typing starts."
  4. Prompt 4: "Localize all new strings. Add i18n keys to all 6 locale files and use data-i18n attributes in HTML and i18n.t() calls in JS."

Show the result after each prompt. Each builds on the last - the audience sees the iteration loop in real time.

Belief #3
"Say no to most features"

When every feature costs months of engineering, "no" is your most important word.

When prototyping costs almost nothing, "no" prevents learning. Instead of debating X vs Y for weeks, prototype both and let users decide.

Cost of debate
is now greater than
Cost of validation

Prototype X

Prototype Y

↓ Run in parallel. Users decide.

Say no to bad problems, not experiments. When validation is cheap, run both.

Jeff Bezos, Amazon shareholder letters: "Double experiments, double inventiveness"

▶ CLICK

"But when prototyping costs almost nothing… 'no' isn't protecting you anymore. It's preventing you from learning. Think about it: instead of debating X versus Y in a meeting for three weeks, what if you just… prototyped both? Put them in front of users. Let them decide."

▶ CLICK

Belief #5
"Data-driven decisions"

"What does the data say?" became the default answer to every product question. Data was the tiebreaker, the decision-maker, the shield.

Data is essential for validation. But it can't tell you what to build next. That takes a point of view. The best PMs use both, but they start with judgment.

Before

Data → Decision

"What does the data say?"

Now

Judgment → Hypothesis → Data validates

"What do we believe? Let's test it."

Lead with judgment, verify with data.

What This Does NOT Mean

✕ Does not mean ✓ Does mean
PMs should replace engineers. → PM and Engg roles converge. Both gain range.
Prototypes go to production unreviewed. → Prototypes earn production. Eng still gates quality.
Every PM must write code. → Every PM can build. Chat is enough to start.
Building replaces talking to customers. → Building is how you talk to customers faster.
The spec is dead. → The spec comes after the prototype, not before.
Newer PMs should skip fundamentals. → Fundamentals are your moat. AI is the force multiplier.

"Now - I know some of you are already thinking of objections. So let me address them head-on before you ask."

"I'm NOT saying PMs should replace engineers. That's not the point. The point is the roles are converging. PMs gain more technical range. Engineers gain more product range. Both get better."

"I'm NOT saying prototypes go straight to production without review. Prototypes earn their way to production. Engineering still gates quality. Always."

"I'm NOT saying every PM must learn to write code. I'm saying every PM can build. And chat - just talking to an AI - is enough to start."

"I'm NOT saying we stop talking to customers. Building IS how you talk to customers - just faster. You put something real in their hands instead of asking them to imagine it."

"I'm NOT saying the spec is dead. The spec just moves. It comes after the prototype, not before it."

"And I am definitely NOT saying newer PMs should skip the fundamentals. Strategy, customer empathy, technical skills, stakeholder management - those are your moat. AI is the force multiplier on top of that foundation."
